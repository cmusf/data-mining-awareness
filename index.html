Data Mining

What is "Data Mining"?

Data mining involves analyzing large sets of data to discover patterns, correlations, and insights. It uses sophisticated algorithms and statistical methods to extract useful information that might not be apparent from a simple observation. This process automated and relies on both historical and current data to make predictions or identify trends[1].

How Does Data Mining Affect You?

The data mining which affects most people, like you and me, often involves the collection of personal information without explicit consent, making it highly invasive. Collected data includes browsing history, purchase records, social media activity, significant locations, and even personal conversations from sharing platforms[2]. This data is analyzed to develop comprehensive profiles of individuals[3][4].

A prime example of the impacts of data mining are covered in Madhumita Murgia's TED talk, where she exposes the alarming extent to which personal data is mined and sold by data brokers, revealing how detailed profiles are created from our online and offline behaviors. She shares her personal journey of discovering the vast amount of information known about her by companies she never interacted with, including her habits, preferences, and movements.

Why Is Our Data Mined?

The primary reason our data is mined is for targeted advertising.[5]

Advertisers use the collected data to understand consumer behavior, preferences, and demographics in a deeply granular way. This information enables them to tailor advertisements to individual users, in hopes to manipulate purchasing decisions. The depth of data collected, including personal habits and interests, allows advertisers to exploit vulnerabilities and influence consumer behavior in ways that may not always be apparent or ethical.

Digital profiles may be sold to private investigators, law enforcement, and anyone else willing to buy.[6]

Who is Mining Our Data?

The largest organizations involved are:

  • Google (Alphabet Inc.)
  • Amazon
  • Facebook
  • Microsoft
  • IBM

All are particularly notorious for their extensive data collection practices[7]. These companies often defend their actions as necessary for enhancing user experience but largely use the data in targeted advertising for the highest bidder.

Evolution of Data Mining

Over the last two decades, data mining has evolved from simple data analysis to complex surveillance mechanisms. The rise of social media and smartphones has led to the collection of even more intimate data, such as real-time location, biometric data (from sensors), and personal preferences.[2] These advancements, have led to unprecedented levels of personal surveillance and data exploitation for commercial gain.

In October of 2019, data broker "People Data Labs" experienced a data leak, containing more than 1.2 records from internet users. The data contained names, jobs, locations, social media accounts, work histories, phone numbers, email addresses, and much more.[9]

What Can I Do to Protect Myself?

To protect yourself invasive data mining, you need to consider who you give your data to.

Consider this: How much does Google know about you?

If you're an average internet user in the US, probably more than you'd like. Google knows who you email, where you work, where you live, when you're online, what you search for, who you visit, who visits you, what your interests are, what your dislikes are, and much, much more.[8]

Once you're aware of the information you give out, it's time to unlink it from your digital identity.

Start by reviewing and adjusting the privacy settings on your social media accounts and other online platforms. Use incognito or private browsing modes when searching online to reduce data tracking. Consider using ad blockers and privacy-focused browsers (such as Mullvad Browser, a privacy-focused browser made in collaboration with the TOR project) and a search engine that does not track your activities. Be cautious about the information you share online, especially in public forums or social media. Educating yourself about data rights and staying informed about how your data is being used are also crucial steps in protecting your digital identity.
